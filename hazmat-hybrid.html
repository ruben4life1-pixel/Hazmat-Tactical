EDUCATION & TRAINING

Understanding the concepts behind the tools makes you a better technician. Here's how to use the calculators effectively and practice real-world scenarios.

üî¨ Understanding PID Correction Factors

PIDs (Photoionization Detectors) are calibrated to isobutylene. Different chemicals ionize differently when exposed to UV light. Without correction, your readings can be dramatically wrong.

Example: Your PID reads 50 ppm at a gasoline spill. Gasoline's correction factor is 2.1. The true concentration is 105 ppm - more than double what the meter shows!

True Concentration = PID Reading √ó Correction Factor

üí• Toxic Before Explosive

Your LEL meter might show "only 10%" - that sounds safe, right? WRONG. That concentration is often dangerously toxic.

Gasoline Example:

  • Toxic threshold: ~300 ppm
  • 10% LEL = 1,400 ppm (4.7x toxic level)
  • 100% LEL = 14,000 ppm (explosive range)

Scenario 1: Industrial Solvent Spill

THE SITUATION:

Unknown chemical spilled in warehouse. Your PID reads 150 ppm. Witness says "smells like paint thinner." Your 4-gas shows 5% LEL.

Questions to Answer:

  • What chemicals smell like paint thinner?
  • Apply correction factors for toluene, xylene, MEK
  • What's the actual ppm if it's toluene (CF: 2.6)?
  • Convert 5% LEL to ppm for each chemical
  • Do your PID and LEL readings agree?

Scenario 2: Vehicle Collision with Fuel Leak

THE SITUATION:

Car vs truck. 10 gallons of gasoline on roadway. Your meters show: PID = 800 ppm, LEL = 15%. Wind from north at 5 mph. ERG says initial isolation 150 ft.

Questions to Answer:

  • Apply CF to PID reading - what's true concentration?
  • Convert 15% LEL to ppm
  • Do your instruments agree?
  • Is this a toxic hazard? (Compare to 300 ppm threshold)
  • Where should command post be positioned?
  • What PPE level is required?

Scenario 3: Ammonia Refrigeration Leak

THE SITUATION:

Food warehouse, ammonia alarm activated. PID reads 300 ppm. Ammonia tube reads 200 ppm. 4-gas shows 0% LEL. Strong ammonia odor.

Questions to Answer:

  • Ammonia CF is 0.8 - what does this mean?
  • Why does PID read HIGHER than actual?
  • Which reading do you trust? (Tube vs PID)
  • Compare to IDLH (300 ppm) - can you enter?
  • Why does LEL show 0%? Is that accurate?

Scenario 4: Unknown Chemical ID

THE SITUATION:

Unlabeled 55-gallon drum leaking. Clear liquid, aromatic smell. Your PID reads 120 ppm. LEL meter shows 6%. You suspect either benzene or toluene.

Questions to Answer:

  • Calculate implied CF from both readings
  • If benzene: 6% of 12,000 ppm = ? ‚Üí CF = ?
  • If toluene: 6% of 12,000 ppm = ? ‚Üí CF = ?
  • Which chemical matches your PID reading?
  • What actions would change if it's benzene vs toluene?

◆ Core Training Principle
At an operations level, your first job is to recognize the hazard — not mitigate it. Container type gives you a probability range before any placard is legible. An oval-section tanker near a gas station is almost always carrying flammable liquids. A ton container inside a water treatment building is almost certainly chlorine. This module sharpens that instinct. When you finish each scenario, open the explanation — the design rationale and common mistakes are where the learning happens.

Scenario 1 — The Fuel Delivery Route
📍
You are responding to an odor complaint on a commercial strip. You observe this tanker pulling into a gas station.
Q1
Based on this container, what type of cargo tank is this?
Q2
Which material is MOST LIKELY transported in this container?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The MC-306/DOT-406 uses a low-profile, non-circular (oval or elliptical) cross-section to lower the center of gravity, reducing rollover risk when carrying dense flammable liquids. Multiple compartments allow the carrier to deliver different fuel grades simultaneously. Aluminum construction keeps weight low for maximum payload.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Gasoline (UN 1203), diesel fuel (UN 1202), ethanol (UN 1170), aviation fuel (UN 1863), and other Class 3 petroleum-based flammable liquids. This is the most frequently encountered cargo tank on American highways.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
OVAL or elliptical cross-section — look at the front or rear of the tank. Multiple dome covers (manholes) on top — typically 4–5, corresponding to compartments. Very low ground clearance. Polished aluminum exterior. Emergency shutoff system visible at rear.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
The most common mistake is confusing this with the MC-307 at a distance. Remember: if the tank appears 'flat and wide' rather than round when viewed from the ends, it's an MC-306. Another mistake is assuming all tankers carry the same product — always look for placards to confirm, but begin hazard assessment from the container shape first.

Q1

Q2

▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The MC-331 is designed to transport compressed or liquefied gases under pressure (typically 100–500 PSI). The perfectly circular cross-section and hemispherical (dome-shaped) ends distribute internal pressure evenly. Thick steel walls handle the pressure load. No bottom outlet is present — all connections are at the top or rear.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Propane (UN 1978), anhydrous ammonia (UN 1005), butane (UN 1011), and other compressed/liquefied gases. In suburban environments, propane delivery is the most common. In agricultural areas, anhydrous ammonia is equally likely.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
ROUND cross-section (circular ends, not oval). HEMISPHERICAL ends — the tank ends curve outward like a dome. High ground clearance (pressurized, thicker walls = less sag). Single rear valve cluster. Typically white or light-colored (heat reflection for pressure management).
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Responders sometimes confuse MC-331 with MC-338 (cryogenic). Key difference: MC-338 has a visibly double-walled outer jacket giving it a polished 'tank within a tank' appearance, with vacuum-jacketed construction and rear valve/fitting assembly. MC-331 looks like a simpler round pressure tank with no outer jacket. Also, never assume only propane — anhydrous ammonia is carried in identical MC-331 tanks.

Scenario 3 — Medical Gas Delivery Truck
📍
An odor complaint at a hospital loading dock. You observe a delivery truck unloading this type of container. The driver says it's 'just medical gas.'
Q1
What type of container is shown?
Q2
Which material is MOST LIKELY in this GREEN-colored cylinder?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
High-pressure gas cylinders are seamless forged steel or aluminum, rated for 2,015–2,400 PSI service. The seamless construction prevents weak points. The hemispherical shoulder tapers to a threaded valve port. A steel protective cap prevents accidental valve damage during transport. The foot ring provides a stable base when standing vertically.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Color coding varies by gas and supplier but common conventions: Green = Oxygen; Yellow = Hydrogen; Gray = CO2; Black/Brown = acetylene (porous media fill). Medical oxygen (UN 1072) is the most common in hospital settings. Industrial cylinders include nitrogen, argon, helium, hydrogen, acetylene, and other compressed gases.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
Vertical cylinder. Dome-shaped shoulder tapering to threaded valve. Steel protective cap (valve guard). Foot ring at base for stable standing. Color coding varies by gas type — never rely on color alone. Look for shoulder stamping: DOT spec, test date, serial number, WC (water capacity), and UN number.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Cylinder color IS a useful initial clue but should NEVER be the sole identifier — color conventions vary by supplier and region. Always verify contents via valve tag, shoulder stamp, or shipping paperwork. Never lay a cylinder on its side without a cradle — the valve can shear. A 'medical gas' statement from a delivery person does not change your response approach until you confirm the specific gas.

Scenario 4 — Warehouse Chemical Pallet
📍
Engine company responds to a 'chemical smell' at a warehouse. You find these containers in the affected area. Labels are visible but difficult to read at your current distance.
Q1
What type of containers are shown on this pallet?
Q2
55-gallon drums like these can carry MANY hazardous materials. Which of the following is a category commonly transported in these drums?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
Steel drums (UN Spec 1A1) are the workhorse of industrial chemical packaging. Rolled seams and body hoops provide structural strength while keeping weight manageable. Two bung plugs on top (2-inch and 3/4-inch) allow filling and venting. UN specification drums are tested for drop, leakage, and stacking loads — the UN/ID marks on the drum indicate what hazard classes it's approved to contain.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Almost anything classified as a hazardous material can be shipped in 55-gallon drums: solvents (acetone, toluene, methanol), acids (sulfuric, hydrochloric, nitric), bases (sodium hydroxide solution), pesticides, paints, lubricating oils, and many others. Drum color and label are the primary identifiers.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
Cylindrical steel body. Two bung plugs on top (one large, one small). Three body hoops (reinforcing rings). UN marking on body side. Label/placard diamond on body. Removable lid versions also exist (open-head drum) for solids and viscous materials.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Responders may underestimate drums because they appear 'small.' However, 55 gallons of sulfuric acid or a flammable solvent is still a significant quantity. Never assume drum contents by color alone. Always read the UN number and proper shipping name from the label before committing to an approach path.

Scenario 5 — Rail Yard Fuel Storage
📍
You are called to a grain elevator with a reported tank car leaking. You observe this tank car on the facility siding.
Q1
What type of rail tank car is shown?
Q2
Which material is MOST COMMONLY transported in DOT-111 general service tank cars?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The DOT-111 (AAR-111) was historically the most common tank car in North America and remains widespread. It is a non-pressure car designed for lower-hazard liquids. Following the 2013 Lac-Mégantic disaster, PHMSA phased DOT-111 cars out of flammable liquid service (crude oil, ethanol) in favor of DOT-117 — but DOT-111 still carries a wide range of non-flammable liquid commodities. Its relatively thin walls keep it lightweight for maximum payload. Bottom outlet valves allow gravity discharge, which is fast but also means product can drain if the outlet is damaged.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Ethanol (UN 1170), corn syrup, vegetable oils, calcium chloride, bleach (sodium hypochlorite), styrene, benzene, crude oil, and many other liquid products. The DOT-111 is the most common tank car in North America — it can carry a very wide range of commodities.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
Long, cylindrical tank with NO protective head shields on the ends (this is the key differentiator). Single loading dome on top center. Bottom outlet valve visible (often protected by a housing). Wheel trucks at roughly 1/4 and 3/4 of car length. Relatively thin-wall appearance compared to pressure cars.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
The #1 error is confusing DOT-111 with DOT-105. The absence of head shields is the definitive difference — if you can clearly see the ends of the tank without a protective bumper/housing, it's most likely a DOT-111. Also, DOT-111 cars can carry flammable liquids — never assume a general service car is carrying something benign.

Scenario 6 — Water Treatment Facility
📍
Dispatched for a reported gas odor at the municipal water treatment plant. You observe this type of container inside the chlorination building through the open door.
Q1
What type of container is shown?
Q2
What material is ALMOST CERTAINLY contained in this type of container at a water treatment plant?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The ton container is designed specifically for high-toxicity compressed/liquefied gases — primarily chlorine and sulfur dioxide. Per CI Pamphlet 1, both valves are located near the center of one end face — the gas valve slightly above centerline, liquid valve slightly below. Fusible plugs are present at both heads. The liquid valve uses an internal eduction tube to draw liquid phase from the bottom of the container.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Chlorine (UN 1017) and sulfur dioxide (UN 1079) are the primary materials in ton containers. Water treatment plants nearly always use chlorine ton containers. Industrial chemical facilities may use SO2 containers. Both are highly toxic gases — chlorine is a pulmonary agent and primary WMD concern.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
HORIZONTAL cylinder. Short and squat proportions (approximately 82 inches long, 30 inches diameter). No wheels — sits in a saddle/cradle. Both valves at ONE end face, near the center — gas valve slightly above centerline, liquid valve slightly below (CI Pamphlet 1). Fusible plugs at both heads. Valve protective housing/bonnet covers both valves during storage and transport. Frost or yellow-green color at the valve end indicates a release.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Responders sometimes confuse ton containers with compressed gas cylinders. Ton containers are ALWAYS horizontal and NEVER stand vertically. The valves are at ONE END of the container — not on top of the body — so your release point is at the head, not midship. Chlorine is 2.5x heavier than air and will seek low areas. ERG Guide 124 distances are dramatically larger for ton containers than for smaller cylinders.

📍
Dispatched for a reported gas leak at a hospital. This tanker is on the loading dock. The driver reports a gauge that is 'behaving oddly.'
Q1
What type of cargo tanker is shown?
Q2
What is the MOST LIKELY material in this type of tanker at a hospital?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The MC-338 uses a vacuum-jacketed double-wall design — essentially a large thermos. The space between the inner and outer tanks is evacuated of air (vacuum insulation), preventing heat transfer by conduction and convection. This keeps cryogenic liquids (at temperatures below -150°F) from warming and expanding. If the vacuum is compromised, the product warms rapidly, pressure builds, and the PRV activates.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Liquid oxygen (UN 1072), liquid nitrogen (UN 1977), liquid argon (UN 1951), liquid hydrogen (UN 1966), and liquid helium. Hospitals receive bulk liquid oxygen. Industrial facilities receive nitrogen and argon. Rocket/aerospace facilities receive liquid hydrogen and helium.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
The POLISHED OUTER SHELL is the primary identifier — visibly bright, stainless steel appearance with a clean double-wall look (inner tank visible through outer jacket gap). No external ring stiffeners. Pressure gauges and valve assembly visible at the rear. May show condensation or ice at rear fittings if there is any product release or a leak. No liquid-level sight glass (vacuum-jacketed design).
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Confusing MC-338 with MC-331 is the critical error. MC-331 is simpler, carries compressed gases at moderate temps, and looks like a basic round-end pressure tank. MC-338's polished double-wall appearance is distinctive once you know it. Also — do NOT apply water to liquid oxygen spills. LOX reacts with organics, can cause fire or explosion, and will be turned from liquid to gas rapidly by water application.

Scenario 8 — Agricultural Supply Dock
📍
Dispatched for a reported chemical spill at a farm supply store. This container is sitting on the dock with a pooling liquid underneath it.
Q1
What type of container is shown?
Q2
Which material is MOST LIKELY in this type of container at an agricultural supply company?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
IBC totes combine a rigid polyethylene inner vessel (for chemical resistance) with a galvanized steel cage (for structural protection) mounted on a standard pallet. The cage allows forklift handling and stacking. The bottom ball valve enables gravity discharge. The poly liner resists a wide range of corrosive materials that would attack steel drums.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Liquid fertilizer solutions, pesticide concentrates, lubricating oils, food-grade liquids, cleaning chemicals, paint, resins, and many other industrial liquids. In agricultural settings, nitrogen-based liquid fertilizer (UAN) and crop protection chemicals are most common.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
Cubic/rectangular outer cage (galvanized steel). White translucent inner container visible through cage. Pallet base (ISO standard footprint). Bottom discharge valve (usually a ball valve). Top fill port with cap. UN label visible on cage side.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
IBC totes look benign but can contain extremely hazardous materials. A tote of concentrated pesticide or a caustic cleaning solution is just as dangerous as a drum of the same chemical. Always read the label and check for UN numbers. The inner poly vessel can fail — check for discoloration, bubbling, or deformation, which indicates chemical incompatibility.

Scenario 9 — Hardware Store Response
📍
Called for a 'gas smell in the parking lot.' A customer points to a shopping cart near the store entrance containing this type of container.
Q1
What type of container is shown?
Q2
What material is in this container?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The 20-lb DOT-4BA cylinder is a low-pressure, welded steel vessel rated for approximately 250 PSI service. Modern cylinders (post-2002) feature the OPD (Overfill Prevention Device) valve, identified by its TRIANGULAR handwheel shape — a critical safety improvement over older hand-wheel designs. The recessed bottom distributes load without a separate foot ring. The collar protects the valve assembly.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Propane (UN 1978) almost exclusively. Some smaller cylinders carry butane (camping stoves), but the standard 20-lb BBQ cylinder contains propane. The nominal 20 lb refers to propane weight, not cylinder weight.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
White or light-colored cylinder. Smaller size (approximately 18 inches tall). TRIANGULAR handwheel on OPD valve (modern cylinders). Collar around valve top for protection. Recessed bottom base (not a foot ring). Water capacity and tare weight stamped on collar.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Responders often underestimate these cylinders because of their ubiquity. However, a 20-lb propane cylinder at a fully involved fire represents a BLEVE risk. The ERG provides specific distances for fire-involved cylinders. Also — the triangular OPD handwheel is a code for 'this is a refillable propane cylinder' — do not confuse it with the fixed-orifice (not refillable) throw-away cylinders used for camp stoves.

Scenario 10 — Chemical Tanker at Industrial Dock
📍
You are on a routine response and observe this tanker at an industrial chemical distributor. The driver is connecting a discharge hose. No placard visible on this side.
Q1
This tanker appears similar to an MC-306 at first glance. What makes this an MC-307/DOT-407 rather than an MC-306/DOT-406?
Q2
Which material is MOST LIKELY transported in this type of container?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The MC-307 is built for a wider range of liquid chemicals than the MC-306. Its round (circular) cross-section and heavier construction handles both flammable and combustible liquids, as well as mild corrosives. The insulating jacket (outer shell) maintains temperature for heat-sensitive products and also provides secondary containment. Low working pressure (3–40 PSI) allows it to handle slightly pressurized or outgassing products.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Chemical solvents, glycerol, corn sweeteners, biodiesel, edible fats and oils, chemical intermediates, mildly corrosive liquids (low-concentration acids or bases), and many other liquids unsuitable for the MC-306.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
ROUND cross-section — the ends are circular, not oval. Single dome cover on top. Outer jacket/shell visible (insulation or secondary containment). Ladder at rear. More upright stance than MC-306 (taller relative to width). No ring stiffeners on the outer surface.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
The MC-306 vs. MC-307 distinction is one of the most common errors in field recognition. The rule is simple: OVAL = MC-306 (fuel), ROUND = MC-307/MC-312 (chemicals). Check the end profile. Also, the single dome cover on an MC-307 contrasts with MC-306's multiple domes — a key secondary cue.

Scenario 11 — Corrosives Tanker on Highway
📍
MVA involving a tanker truck. The tanker is involved in a rear-end collision and has minor body damage. No placard visible but driver says it's 'acid.'
Q1
You observe EXTERNAL RING STIFFENERS visible on the tank body. What type of cargo tank does this indicate?
Q2
Based on this container type, which material is the MOST LIKELY match?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The MC-312 is specifically designed for corrosive materials. The external ring stiffeners are the load-bearing design element — they allow thinner tank walls (which reduces weight) while maintaining structural strength against external impact and internal pressure. The thinner walls are lined with corrosion-resistant material (stainless steel, fiberglass, or rubber lining) appropriate for the acid being carried.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Sulfuric acid (UN 1830), hydrochloric acid (UN 1789), nitric acid (UN 2031), phosphoric acid (UN 1805), sodium hydroxide solution (UN 1824), and other corrosive liquids are the primary cargo. Fertilizer-grade ammonium nitrate solution is also carried in this type.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
ROUND cross-section. EXTERNAL RING STIFFENERS visible around the circumference — these look like ribs or hoops welded to the outside. Smaller diameter than typical MC-307. White or stainless steel appearance. Small manway on top. No bottom outlet (most designs).
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Many responders don't know the MC-312's defining feature: the external ring stiffeners. Without this knowledge, MC-312 and MC-307 look nearly identical. Inspect the tank BODY for external ribs — if you see them, the probability of corrosive cargo jumps significantly. Treat the approach zone accordingly: corrosives have no visible vapor cloud but will cause immediate tissue damage on contact.

Scenario 12 — Pressure Rail Car at Chemical Plant
📍
Called for a 'hissing noise' at a chemical plant's rail siding. You observe this rail car. You can see the ends of the car clearly.
Q1
What is the KEY identifying feature that distinguishes this from a DOT-111 general service car?
Q2
Which material is MOST LIKELY in a DOT-105 pressure car at a chemical plant?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The DOT-105 is a pressure-rated rail car built to contain gases that are toxic, flammable, or both. The thick tank walls (thicker than DOT-111) are covered with protective head shields at both ends — these shields protect the critical dome valve cluster from damage in a derailment. All connections are at the top (no bottom outlet) to prevent catastrophic product loss if the car rolls over.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Chlorine (UN 1017), anhydrous ammonia (UN 1005), vinyl chloride (UN 1086), hydrogen chloride (UN 1050), and other toxic or flammable compressed gases. Chlorine rail cars are almost exclusively DOT-105 or DOT-105A cars.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
PROTECTIVE HEAD SHIELDS on both ends — a distinct 'bumper' or housing around the tank ends. TOP FITTINGS ONLY — no bottom outlet valve. Shorter/fatter appearance than DOT-111 for the same car length (thicker walls). All loading/unloading connections at the top dome area.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
The absent head shield on DOT-111 vs. the present head shield on DOT-105 is the critical field differentiation. At a rail yard, this distinction determines whether you are dealing with a possible corn syrup leak (DOT-111) or a possible chlorine release (DOT-105). Getting this wrong has killed responders. Burn the image of the head shield into your memory.

Scenario 13 — Container Port Intermodal Yard
📍
Dispatched for a 'fuel smell' at a container terminal. You observe a single container on a chassis being moved. This is what you see.
Q1
What type of container is shown?
Q2
ISO intermodal tank containers can carry an extremely wide range of materials. Given only the container type (no placard visible), what is your BEST initial size-up approach?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
ISO tanks conform to international shipping standards, allowing the same container to travel on ships, rail cars, and highway chassis without changing tanks. The cylindrical tank is mounted inside a rigid ISO frame with four corner castings. The frame absorbs loading and impact forces, protecting the tank. ISO tanks are classified by maximum working pressure (T1–T22 codes), with higher T numbers for higher pressures and more hazardous cargoes.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Virtually any liquid or gas: food-grade products (fruit juice, wine, edible oils), industrial chemicals (acids, bases, solvents), petrochemicals, liquefied gases (in specialized versions), and many others. The ISO tank is a universal container — cargo identification requires label/placard or shipping documents.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
RECTANGULAR ISO frame with corner castings at all 8 corners. Cylindrical tank INSIDE the frame. Fittings on top and bottom. International dimensions (20-ft standard). UN marks and T-code on frame plate.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Responders at port settings may assume ISO tanks are simply cargo containers like standard dry boxes. This error can be fatal — ISO tanks can and do contain chlorine, anhydrous ammonia, concentrated acids, and other highly hazardous materials. The ISO frame looks like a regular shipping container from a distance. Confirm: is there a cylinder inside the frame? If yes, treat as potential hazmat until confirmed otherwise.

Scenario 14 — Industrial Gas Supplier Incident
📍
Dispatched for an equipment failure at an industrial gas supplier. You observe this vehicle in the loading bay.
Q1
What type of container/vehicle is shown?
Q2
Which material is MOST LIKELY on a tube trailer at an industrial gas supplier?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
Tube trailers use multiple seamless steel cylinders (tubes) mounted horizontally on a trailer frame. Each tube is rated for 2,000–6,000 PSI. Multiple tubes are connected to a common manifold, allowing combined capacity to be dispensed from a single outlet. This design is used for gases that are difficult or uneconomical to liquefy (like hydrogen and helium) or for compressed natural gas (CNG) distribution.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Hydrogen (UN 1049), compressed natural gas/methane (UN 1971), helium (UN 1046), argon (UN 1006), oxygen (UN 1072), nitrogen (UN 1066), and other permanent or difficult-to-liquefy gases. Hydrogen tube trailers are increasingly common with hydrogen fuel cell infrastructure growth.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
Multiple long tubes (cylinders) mounted horizontally and in parallel on the trailer. Front manifold connecting all tubes. Rear manifold with pressure gauges and discharge connections. No single tank shell — the tubes are the containers. High-pressure appearance (thick walls, robust manifold construction).
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Tube trailers at first glance might look like a flatbed with cargo. The 'cargo' IS the containers. Key recognition: parallel cylinders running the full length of the trailer, with manifold connections at both ends. Hydrogen tube trailers require extreme isolation distances due to hydrogen's extraordinarily wide flammable range (4–75%) and very low ignition energy.

Scenario 15 — Rural Agricultural Accident
📍
Dispatched for a farm equipment accident with a reported chemical release. You find a tractor that has tipped over, pulling a trailer-mounted tank with it. The white tank is on its side and you can smell something acrid.
Q1
Before it tipped, what type of container was this?
Q2
Based on the container type, location, and odor description, what material is MOST LIKELY involved?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
Agricultural nurse tanks are smaller pressure vessels (typically 500–3,000 gallons) designed specifically for anhydrous ammonia application in farming. They are trailer-mounted and towed by a tractor to the field. Unlike an MC-331 which delivers product to a stationary tank, the nurse tank IS the mobile applicator — it feeds directly to injection knives that inject NH3 into the soil.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Almost exclusively Anhydrous Ammonia (UN 1005) — hence the informal name 'ammonia nurse tank.' These tanks are found at farm cooperatives, on farm trailer hitches, and in fields throughout the agricultural Midwest, Great Plains, and other farming regions.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
Similar in shape to a smaller MC-331 — round, white, pressurized cylinder. Smaller than highway MC-331 (typically 1,000–3,000 gallons vs. 9,000+ gallons). Trailer mounted, low to the ground. Applicator hose connections and valve assembly visible on the rear. Agricultural equipment context (farm field, tractor, cooperative).
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Many responders in urban areas have never seen a nurse tank and may misidentify it as a propane tank or industrial gas vessel. In agricultural settings, the acrid smell of ammonia + a white pressurized tank on a farm trailer = anhydrous ammonia until proven otherwise. Anhydrous ammonia is immediately dangerous to life and health (IDLH: 300 ppm). ERG Guide 125 — aggressive isolation distances.
TIER 2 — INTERMEDIATE #16
Scenario 16 — Construction Site Chemical Delivery
📍
Dispatched for a container leaking at a concrete manufacturing plant. The dispatch caller says 'it's a big truck with hoppers on it.'
Q1
What type of trailer is shown?
Q2
Given this is at a concrete/cement plant, what material is MOST LIKELY in this type of trailer?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
Dry bulk pneumatic trailers use V-shaped hoppers that allow gravity concentration of dry material at the discharge cone. Pneumatic air pressure is then used to push the powder through pipes to storage silos. The top loading hatches are large enough to receive product from overhead conveyors or augers. This design CANNOT carry liquids — it is engineered exclusively for pourable dry solids.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Cement (corrosive dust), fly ash, dry lime (calcium oxide — UN 1910, corrosive), dry ammonium nitrate fertilizer, plastic pellets, flour, dry caustic soda, and other pourable solids. In cement/concrete settings, quicklime (CaO) is extremely hazardous — it reacts violently with water and moisture.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
V-shaped or cone-bottom hoppers (the characteristic inverted pyramid shape). Top loading hatches. Pneumatic discharge pipes at rear/bottom. No liquid connections. Rectangular cross-section profile (not cylindrical).
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Dry does NOT mean safe. Dry lime and calcium oxide are severe corrosives — contact with moist eyes or skin can cause serious chemical burns. Inhaling cement or lime dust causes acute respiratory injury. Also — dry ammonium nitrate in a bulk trailer is a detonation hazard if involved in fire. Treat dry bulk cargo as unknown hazmat until shipping documents are confirmed.

Scenario 17 — University Laboratory Emergency
📍
Called for a gas smell near a university chemistry building. A graduate student points to this container, saying it was tipped over. The area smells cold and there is frost on the floor near the container.
Q1
What type of container is shown?
Q2
The frost and 'cold smell' near the tipped Dewar suggests this MOST LIKELY contains:
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The portable Dewar uses the same vacuum-jacket insulation principle as the MC-338 tanker but at a much smaller, portable scale. The wide mouth and foam-stopper cap (or a pressure-relief valve depending on version) maintains cryogenic temperatures. Open Dewars (wide mouth, foam stopper) are for dipping/retrieval. Pressure-buildup Dewars (valve on top, gauge visible) can supply gas at controlled pressure.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Liquid nitrogen (LN2 — UN 1977), liquid argon (LAr — UN 1951), liquid oxygen (LOX — UN 1072), liquid carbon dioxide, and liquid helium. In laboratory settings, LN2 is by far the most common — used for biological sample preservation, cooling, and cryo-applications. Liquid oxygen is used in medical and research settings.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
Shorter, wider cylinder compared to a gas cylinder. Polished stainless outer shell (vacuum jacket). Pressure gauge and valve/vent on top. Handle on side. Rubber bumper rings. Foot ring. No color coding requirement (unlike compressed gas cylinders).
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
A tipped Dewar in an enclosed room is an oxygen-deficiency emergency. Liquid nitrogen boils off into nitrogen gas — 160 liters of LN2 becomes approximately 113,000 liters of nitrogen gas, rapidly displacing oxygen. An enclosed space can become IMMEDIATELY DANGEROUS within minutes. Never assume 'just nitrogen' is harmless — confined space asphyxiation is the primary risk. Confirm O2 levels with a meter before entry.
TIER 2 — INTERMEDIATE #18
Scenario 18 — Rail Yard LP Gas Storage
📍
Dispatched for a 'hissing sound' from the rail yard. You observe this car on a siding. You can see protective structures on both ends of the car.
Q1
You observe this rail car has HEAD SHIELDS on both ends like a DOT-105, but it appears longer. What does this suggest?
Q2
Based on DOT-112 classification, which material is MOST LIKELY in this rail car?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The DOT-112 is a pressure rail car specifically designed for LP gases (propane, butane, and related hydrocarbons). Like the DOT-105, it has protective head shields. However, the DOT-112 typically has a higher capacity (larger diameter and/or length) than the DOT-105 because LP gases, while flammable, are lower toxicity than materials like chlorine. All fittings are at the top only.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
Propane (UN 1978), butane (UN 1011), isobutane (UN 1969), propylene (UN 1077), and other LP gas products. These cars are found at LP gas terminals, refineries, and petrochemical facilities.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
HEAD SHIELDS present (like DOT-105). Top-only fittings (like DOT-105). Generally LONGER than DOT-105. The key distinction between DOT-105 (toxic gases) and DOT-112 (LP gases) is largely a matter of the specific chemical — both have head shields. ERG Guide 115 (flammable gas) vs. 124/125 (toxic) becomes critical.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Confusing DOT-105 (chlorine, ammonia) with DOT-112 (propane) changes your entire response. Both have head shields and top-only fittings. The distinction requires confirmation from placards, railroad documentation, or the car's tank spec plate. In the absence of that information: if it has head shields and is at a petrochemical/LP terminal, DOT-112 is more likely. If it's at a water treatment or chemical plant, DOT-105 is more likely. When in doubt — ERG Guide 111 and maximum isolation until confirmed.
TIER 2 — INTERMEDIATE #19
Scenario 19 — Differentiation Challenge
📍
You are reviewing scene photos from an industrial accident. Two tankers are on scene. Your dispatcher wants to know if they are the same type or different, and why.
Q1
Looking at Tanker A (left) and Tanker B (right), what is the DEFINITIVE visual difference that distinguishes their type?
Q2
Tanker B (with ring stiffeners) is an MC-312. What hazard class cargo is it MOST LIKELY carrying?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
The MC-307 and MC-312 are both round cross-section tankers, making them visually similar at distance. The MC-312's external ring stiffeners allow for thinner tank walls (saving weight) while providing equivalent structural integrity. The thin walls are protected by corrosion-resistant linings (stainless steel, fiberglass, rubber, or HDPE) appropriate for the specific acid or base being transported.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
MC-307: Chemical solvents, glycerol, edible oils, moderate chemicals. MC-312: Sulfuric acid, hydrochloric acid, nitric acid, phosphoric acid, sodium hydroxide solutions, and other corrosive liquids are its primary cargo.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
The definitive differentiator: RING STIFFENERS on the MC-312's outer surface. These are external metal rings welded around the circumference of the tank. They are clearly visible and unmistakable when you know what to look for. MC-307 has a smooth outer shell.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Many experienced responders miss this distinction because both look like 'round chemical tankers.' Develop a habit: when you see a round tanker, immediately look for external rings on the body. If present = MC-312 = likely corrosive. If absent = MC-307 = likely chemical but not necessarily corrosive. This single observation changes your PPE selection and approach angle.

Scenario 20 — Limited View from Road Overpass
📍
You are the first arriving officer. While positioning, you observe a rail car through a chain-link fence from a road overpass. You can only see the END of the car. The car is on a siding adjacent to a chemical plant. It is nighttime.
Q1
From this LIMITED END VIEW, what observable features would indicate this is MOST LIKELY a pressure car (DOT-105) rather than a general service car (DOT-111)?
Q2
Without placard confirmation, at a CHEMICAL PLANT siding, what is the MOST APPROPRIATE initial action while awaiting hazmat team arrival?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
At distance, end-view analysis of a rail car can still yield useful information. The circular profile of a pressure car end is typically larger in diameter (wider) relative to its visible length than a general service car. The presence of head shields — even partially visible — narrows the car type significantly. Top-only fittings are visible even from some distance.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
At a chemical plant siding, the probability of a pressure car containing toxic gas (chlorine, ammonia, hydrogen chloride, vinyl chloride) is significantly elevated compared to a grain elevator or food processor. Context matters enormously in operations-level recognition.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
End-profile analysis: Large circular end + visible protective arc/housing around end = probable pressure car. Top-only valve fitting cluster visible = pressure car indicator. No visible bottom outlet = pressure car indicator. Combination of these features at a chemical plant = TREAT AS TOXIC GAS until confirmed otherwise.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
The most dangerous mistake in this scenario is letting urgency override caution. Approaching a possibly chlorine-loaded DOT-105 to read the spec plate — without full SCBA and appropriate PPE — because 'it might be nothing' has contributed to responder fatalities. Establish maximum isolation, make notifications, and let confirmation come to you rather than approaching the hazard.
TIER 3 — ADVANCED #21
Scenario 21 — MVA with Overturned Tanker
📍
You arrive at a reported highway accident. A tanker truck has overturned. The tanker is inverted — bottom visible. There is no visible spill. No placard visible from your approach angle. Bystanders are stopped and watching from approximately 50 feet away.
Q1
From the BOTTOM VIEW of an overturned tanker, you can observe a visible bottom outlet valve. What does this tell you about the container type?
Q2
The overturned tanker has an OVAL cross-section visible at the front cab area. There is no spill yet. What is your MOST IMPORTANT IMMEDIATE action?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
An overturned tanker presents the bottom face (normally hidden) to responders. Bottom outlet valves are present on MC-306 and MC-307/MC-312 cargo tanks but NOT on MC-331 pressure tanks or rail cars. The oval cross-section visible from the ends definitively identifies this as an MC-306 — the most common highway tanker. MC-306 carries flammable liquids almost exclusively.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
With an oval cross-section confirmed: gasoline (UN 1203), diesel (UN 1202), ethanol (UN 1170), and other petroleum products are the primary cargoes. An overturned MC-306 near ignition sources is a major fire risk even before any product is visible.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
Bottom outlet valve visible (confirms non-pressure tank). Oval cross-section visible at ends (confirms MC-306 not MC-307). Multiple dome cover openings visible along the tank top (now pointing downward). Emergency shutoff system visible on bottom side.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
The distraction in this scenario is the absent placard. Responders sometimes freeze, trying to get a placard number before acting. The container shape itself — oval = MC-306 = flammable liquid — justifies immediate perimeter establishment and bystander removal. You do not need the placard to make that protective action decision. You need the placard to make the detailed ERG reference call.
TIER 3 — ADVANCED #22
Scenario 22 — Partially Obscured Industrial Tanker
📍
You are called to investigate a 'chemical smell' at an industrial distribution center. You can see a tanker truck through a partially open loading bay door. Overhead pipes block your view of the top of the tank. The right end of the tanker is behind a building corner. You cannot see any placards.
Q1
From the VISIBLE PORTION of the tank body, you can see EXTERNAL RING STIFFENERS on the surface. This is the ONLY feature visible. What does this single feature tell you?
Q2
Knowing this is probably an MC-312, and the call is for a 'chemical smell,' what is the MOST APPROPRIATE operations-level action?
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
Advanced scenarios often present partial information. The goal is to NARROW POSSIBILITIES using visible features. External ring stiffeners are NOT present on MC-306, MC-307, or MC-331. They are the defining structural feature of the MC-312. This single visible feature provides high-confidence identification even when other features are obscured.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
MC-312 corrosive cargo: sulfuric acid, hydrochloric acid (which would cause a chemical smell — HCl fumes), nitric acid, sodium hydroxide, and other corrosives are the primary suspects. HCl vapor from a damaged tank would produce exactly the type of irritating 'chemical smell' described in the scenario.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
Focus on what IS visible. Visible ring stiffeners = MC-312 = corrosive. Upwind approach angle. The fact that you cannot see the ends or placards should trigger maximum caution, not paralysis.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
The common error in this scenario is assuming that because you can't see everything, you can't make any useful decision. You CAN make a decision based on what IS visible. Ring stiffeners = corrosive probability = appropriate protective action before you have full information. Responders who wait for 'certainty' before acting may be waiting downwind of a sulfuric acid vapor release.
TIER 3 — ADVANCED #23
Scenario 23 — Pre-Dawn Rail Yard Silhouette
📍
You are called for a rail car 'hissing' in a freight yard at 4 AM. Lighting is limited. You observe a silhouette of a rail car end from approximately 200 feet. You can make out the end profile of the car and what appears to be a structure around the end of the car. No lights are close enough to illuminate the placards. No rail yard workers are accessible.
Q1
Using ONLY the visible silhouette features (circular end profile + apparent protective structure around the car end), what is the SAFEST identification choice?
Q2
Hissing from a rail car at a freight yard adjacent to a chemical manufacturing facility at 4 AM. You cannot confirm the product. Your operations-level action is:
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
Night and limited visibility scenarios require heightened caution precisely because you have fewer identification tools. The combination of: (1) chemical plant context, (2) pressure car silhouette features, and (3) audible hissing elevates the hazard probability enough to justify maximum protective action.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
At a chemical plant rail siding, pressure cars containing chlorine (UN 1017), anhydrous ammonia (UN 1005), or vinyl chloride (UN 1086) are all plausible. Each has dramatically different ERG distances. In the absence of product confirmation, ERG Guide 111 (Unknown Hazmat) + worst-case assumption drives initial action.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
At night, silhouette analysis is the primary tool. A circular end profile + visible protective structure = probable pressure car. Compare with a non-pressured car: DOT-111 ends would appear as plain curved cylindrical ends without the visible 'housing' around them.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Two opposing errors are common here: (1) Overconfidence — 'it's probably just a relief valve on a propane car' — leads to inadequate isolation and possible mass casualty. (2) Paralysis — 'I can't see the placard so I can't do anything' — leaves personnel in a possibly hazardous atmosphere. The correct answer is action based on worst-case assumption while seeking confirmation. This is the operations-level framework.

Scenario 24 — Damaged IBC with Partial Label
📍
Dispatched to a warehouse for a 'chemical odor.' On arrival, you find a damaged IBC tote on a pallet. The cage is bent and the inner plastic vessel is cracked. A liquid is pooling under the tote. The label is partially torn — you can see: 'UN 18' (rest torn off). One worker has skin irritation on her arm. She says it 'burned immediately when it splashed.'
Q1
The partial UN number 'UN 18__' combined with immediate burn sensation on skin contact MOST LIKELY narrows this to what hazard class?
Q2
The injured worker is ambulatory. Your FIRST priority as operations-level is:
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
IBC totes in warehouse and industrial settings frequently contain bulk quantities of liquid chemicals. The combination of container type, partial UN number, injury pattern (immediate burn on skin contact), and industrial context provides enough operational information to make a protective action decision.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
UN 18__ corrosives include: UN 1830 (sulfuric acid), UN 1789 (hydrochloric acid solution), UN 1823 (sodium hydroxide solid), UN 1824 (sodium hydroxide solution), UN 1814 (potassium hydroxide solution), and others. All cause immediate chemical burns on skin contact.
🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION
In this scenario, the key recognition elements are contextual: container type (IBC = industrial bulk liquid), partial label, and victim presentation. Immediate burn on skin contact from a pooling clear-to-yellowish liquid = corrosive until proven otherwise.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Waiting for exact chemical ID before initiating gross decon for a confirmed chemical burn is a critical error. For corrosives, the primary treatment (copious water irrigation) is the same regardless of which specific acid or base is involved. Immediate decon reduces burn severity — every 30-second delay matters. Begin decon concurrent with hazmat ID efforts, not after.

Scenario 25 — Night Scene, Limited Resources
📍
You are the first arriving unit at 2 AM to a reported single-vehicle accident on a rural highway. You see an overturned tanker in the ditch. One person is visible near the truck, apparently ejected. The tanker shape is visible in your headlights. You can see ONLY the front end of the tanker — it is round. Hazmat is 45 minutes away. You have one engine company (4 personnel).
Q1
The round END PROFILE visible in your headlights tells you this is NOT an MC-306. What does this inform your size-up?
Q2
You have ONE person apparently ejected near the overturned round-end tanker. 45 minutes to hazmat. As operations-level IC, your decisions in the first 5 minutes are:
▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
This is the hardest operations-level scenario — a viable victim near an unknown hazmat container with limited resources and delayed hazmat. The correct answer is NOT 'do nothing' — but it requires rapid, calibrated risk analysis.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS
A round-end tanker on a rural highway at night could be: MC-307 (chemical solvent — moderate hazard), MC-312 (corrosive — serious hazard), MC-331 (propane or anhydrous ammonia — serious hazard). These four observations take 30 seconds and inform everything.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES
Two classic errors: (1) Paralysis — 'I can't do anything until hazmat arrives' — leaves a viable victim to die. (2) Rushing — 'the victim is our priority, forget the hazmat' — kills the rescuer and the victim. The trained operations-level answer is deliberate, PPE-protected, upwind, rapid assessment with awareness of the hazard probability. This is exactly what operations-level training prepares you for.

📍
Called to a container terminal for an odor complaint. Dock workers have evacuated one area. You see a single ISO tank container on a chassis, rear panel open, with a puddle under the rear sump valve. No placard is visible from your approach angle. One dock worker says 'the manifest is in the office, but the office is closed.' You are 300 feet away.
Q1
ISO tank containers can contain substances ranging from fruit juice to chlorine. From 300 feet with no placard visible and a puddle under the sump valve, your INITIAL HAZARD ASSUMPTION should be:

▶ SHOW EXPLANATION Click to expand instructor notes
⚒ CONTAINER DESIGN
Port settings add complexity — multiple container types, international cargo, limited on-site hazmat information, and potentially dozens of dockworkers in the area. ISO tanks at ports carry everything from wine to concentrated industrial chemicals. The container ID number on the frame can be looked up through the shipping line to get manifest information.
☢ TYPICAL MATERIALS

🔎 VISUAL IDENTIFICATION

Port incidents often create a false sense of normalcy — the area looks busy and industrial, and dockworkers are often nearby doing routine work. This normalcy bias can delay appropriate isolation.
